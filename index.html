What is EMI?

The Equated Monthly Installment, or EMI, is the sum paid each month to the bank or other financial institution until the debt is fully paid off. It includes both interest on the loan and a portion of the principal amount to be returned. The sum of principal and interest is divided by the loan's tenure, or the number of months it must be repaid in. This sum must be paid monthly. The interest component of the EMI would be higher in the early months and subsequently decrease with each payment. The exact percentage given to principle payments is determined by the interest rate. Although your monthly EMI payment will remain same, the proportion of principle and interest components. Will change over time. With each subsequent payment, you will contribute more to the principal and less to interest.

You would need to pay ₹13,493 for 120 months to settle the whole loan amount. The total amount payable is ₹13,493 * 120 = ₹16,19,220, including ₹6,19,220 as loan interest.

Floating Rate EMI Calculation

We recommend that you compute floating / variable rate EMI using two opposite scenarios: optimistic (deflationary) and pessimistic (inflationary). Loan amount and loan tenure, the two components necessary to calculate the EMI, are under your control; that is, you will pick how much loan you can borrow and how long your loan term should be. However, banks and HFCs establish their own interest rates based on the RBI's rates and regulations. As a borrower, you should evaluate the two extremes of an increase or fall in interest rates and calculate your EMI based on these two scenarios. This calculation will help you decide how much EMI is affordable, how long your loan term should be, and how much you should borrow.

Optimistic (deflationary) scenario: Assume that interest rates fall by 1% to 3% from their current levels. Consider this scenario and determine your EMI. In this case, your EMI will decrease, or you may choose to shorten the loan term. Ex: If you take out a home loan to buy a property as an investment, an optimistic scenario allows you to compare it to other investment alternatives.

Pessimistic (inflationary) scenario: Assume that interest rates increase by 1% - 3%. Is it possible for you to continue paying the EMI without any difficulty? Even a 2% increase in interest rate can result in a considerable increase in monthly payments throughout the life of the loan.

Such a calculation allows you to plan for such future scenarios. When you take out a loan, you make a financial commitment for the next several months, years, even decades. So prepare for both the best and worst-case scenarios. In short, hope for the best, but prepare for the worse!
